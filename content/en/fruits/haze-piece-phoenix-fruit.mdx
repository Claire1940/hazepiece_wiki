<Section icon={Swords} title="Phoenix Fruit Move Breakdown">

Phoenix Fruit’s move set is built around ranged pressure, a movement burst, a heal, and a finisher-style impact sequence. That mix makes it flexible, but only if you understand what each move is for. The biggest mistake is trying to treat every button as a raw damage button.

| Move Type | Best Use | Strength | Caution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ranged flame attack | Tagging targets from safety | **Good reach** | Don’t rely on it alone |
| Spiral thrust | Closing distance or chasing | **Strong mobility-damage hybrid** | Easy to overcommit |
| Healing flame | Resetting after a trade | **Excellent sustain** | Save it for real damage windows |
| Slamming impact | Burst pressure at close range | **Strong combat tool** | Best after you force movement |
| Heavenly tornado | Area control and finishing | **Useful zone pressure** | Stronger when enemies are pinned |

<Callout type="warning" title="Important Limitation">
The fruit’s base form does not carry the same weight as the transformed kit. If you enter combat without a plan, you can feel underpowered very quickly.
</Callout>

A good way to read the kit is simple:
- **Ranged move** for setup.
- **Spiral-style thrust** for commitment or chase.
- **Healing move** for recovery after a trade.
- **Impact and tornado tools** for finishers and space control.

That order matters because Phoenix Fruit performs best when you decide the pace of the fight. If the opponent controls the rhythm, your weaker base phase becomes a liability. If you control spacing, the fruit starts to feel much more oppressive.

| Move Priority | When to Press It | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Opening range check | Safe pressure |
| 2 | Enemy dodge or gap-closing | Chase or punish |
| 3 | After taking damage | Sustain reset |
| 4 | Enemy is trapped or forced in | Burst finisher |

The safest mindset is to use each move as part of a short exchange cycle, not a solo answer to everything. That keeps the fruit efficient and reduces the risk of burning your strongest tool at the wrong time.

<Section icon={Target} title="How to Use Phoenix Fruit in PvP and Bossing">

Phoenix Fruit is at its best when you play a controlled, mid-range game. You want to enter fights with enough spacing to avoid free punishment, then transform and commit only after you understand the enemy’s reaction pattern. That approach works far better than trying to brute-force every encounter.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Transform Before Commitment">
    Enter the transformed form before you start your main engagement. The fruit’s real value sits behind its Phoenix state, so treat transformation as the opening move.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Open From Safe Range">
    Start with the long-range flame tool or aerial movement. This lets you test defenses without giving up your position.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Trade, Then Reset">
    After you land damage, use the heal to stabilize. A successful Phoenix player is usually the one who wins the second exchange, not the first.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Finish With Impact Pressure">
    Use the stronger impact-style and tornado-style tools when the enemy has already spent movement or is forced to stand still.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Callout type="info" title="Best Fight Pattern">
Use Phoenix Fruit like a rhythm tool: poke, reposition, heal, then re-engage. That loop is more reliable than trying to win every exchange with raw burst.
</Callout>

For boss fights, patience matters even more. If a boss has a clear attack window, save your stronger damage for after the pattern ends. Phoenix does not need reckless overextension to succeed. It needs timing, and it rewards players who understand when to stop pressing forward.

In team fights, your role is similar: move in and out, punish isolated targets, and avoid being the first body on the line. The fruit’s flight makes that playstyle easier because it gives you a clean way to reset your angle.
